THREAT CATALOG

The six malware types worth knowing

"Malware" is an umbrella term. Each type below has its own way of spreading and its own goal — knowing the difference helps you spot the warning signs faster.

T-01

Virus

Attaches itself to a legitimate file or program and activates when that host file is opened, then copies itself into other files.

Spreads via: infected files, email attachments, shared drives
Needs: a human to run the host program
T-02

Worm

A self-contained program that copies itself across networks on its own — no host file, no click required after the first infection.

Spreads via: network connections, unpatched vulnerabilities
Needs: nothing — it's fully self-replicating
T-03

Trojan

Disguises itself as legitimate software. Once installed, it quietly opens a back door for attackers instead of doing what it claimed to do.

Spreads via: fake apps, cracked software, deceptive downloads
Needs: the user to willingly install it
T-04

Ransomware

Encrypts your files and demands payment for the decryption key. Even paying offers no guarantee your files come back.

Spreads via: phishing emails, exposed remote access, exploits
Needs: a foothold on the device or network
T-05

Spyware

Runs silently in the background, logging keystrokes, browsing habits, or credentials and sending them back to an attacker.

Spreads via: bundled downloads, malicious links, fake apps
Needs: to stay hidden to keep collecting data
T-06

Adware

Floods your device with unwanted ads and pop-ups. Often more annoying than dangerous, but frequently bundled with spyware.

Spreads via: free software bundles, browser extensions
Needs: installation alongside another program
ATTACK PATH

How malware actually gets onto a device

A typical infection follows a short, predictable chain. Recognizing each link is often enough to break it.

STEP 1

Attacker sends a lure — email, link, ad, or fake update

→
STEP 2

User clicks, downloads, or installs

→
STEP 3

Malicious code executes on the device

→
STEP 4

Malware spreads, hides, or phones home

Phishing emails

Fake messages that impersonate a trusted sender to get you to click a link or open an attachment.

Malicious downloads

Pirated software, cracked games, and "free" tools that bundle hidden payloads.

Infected USB drives

Plugging in an unknown drive can trigger auto-run scripts that install malware silently.

Drive-by downloads

Just visiting a compromised website can silently install malware through a browser flaw.

Unpatched software

Known vulnerabilities in outdated apps or operating systems give attackers a ready-made way in.

Fake update prompts

Pop-ups claiming your browser or player "needs an update" often install malware instead.

SCAN LOG — HISTORICAL

Real-world outbreaks

These incidents shaped how the world thinks about malware — and each teaches a different lesson.

2017RANSOMWARE

WannaCry

Spread through an unpatched Windows vulnerability and hit roughly 200,000 computers across some 150 countries in days, disrupting hospitals, factories, and transit systems. Lesson: unpatched systems are an open door.

2000VIRUS / WORM

ILOVEYOU

Arrived as an email attachment disguised as a love letter. Once opened, it overwrote files and emailed itself to every contact, causing billions of dollars in estimated global damage. Lesson: curiosity is an attack vector.

2016BOTNET / WORM

Mirai Botnet

Hijacked poorly secured smart cameras and routers using their default passwords, then used them to knock major websites offline in one of the largest DDoS attacks recorded. Lesson: change default device passwords.

2017WIPER / WORM

NotPetya

Disguised as ransomware but actually designed to destroy data permanently. It crippled shipping, logistics, and manufacturing firms worldwide, with damage estimates reaching billions of dollars. Lesson: backups are the only real recovery plan.

2010WORM / SABOTAGE

Stuxnet

A highly targeted worm that spread via USB drives and specifically sabotaged industrial equipment, showing that malware can cause damage in the physical world, not just on screens. Lesson: even isolated systems need defense.

WARNING SIGNS

Signs your device may be infected

No single symptom proves an infection, but more than one at once is worth investigating.

Device suddenly runs much slower than usual

Unexpected pop-ups appear even when offline

Battery drains far faster than normal

Unfamiliar apps, toolbars, or icons appear

Frequent crashes, freezes, or restarts

Sudden spike in mobile or network data use

Antivirus or security settings disable themselves

Contacts report strange messages from your accounts

WHAT'S AT STAKE

What you actually stand to lose

Malware isn't just an inconvenience — it puts real things on the line.

Identity theft

Stolen personal details used to open accounts or commit fraud in your name.

Financial loss

Drained bank accounts, fraudulent charges, or ransom payments with no guarantee of recovery.

Permanent data loss

Photos, documents, and projects encrypted or destroyed with no way to get them back.

Privacy exposure

Private messages, photos, or webcam access exposed to an attacker or leaked publicly.

Account takeover

Attackers locking you out of email, banking, or social accounts using stolen credentials.

Work disruption

Lost productivity or halted operations while systems are cleaned and restored.

YOUR DEFENSE PLAN

Prevention checklist

Tap each item as you put it in place. Most infections are stopped by a small handful of consistent habits — not fancy tools.

PROTECTION SCORE
0 / 10

Keep software updated

Turn on automatic updates for your OS, browser, and apps to close known vulnerabilities.

Use reputable antivirus

Run real-time protection from a trusted vendor and let it scan on a schedule.

Back up your data (3-2-1 rule)

3 copies, 2 different media, 1 stored offline or off-site — so ransomware has no leverage.

Use strong, unique passwords + MFA

A password manager plus multi-factor authentication stops most account takeovers cold.

Pause before clicking

Hover over links, verify senders, and never open unexpected attachments.

Download only from official sources

Stick to official app stores and vendor websites — skip cracked software entirely.

Enable your firewall

Keep the built-in firewall on for every device that connects to the internet.

Review app permissions

Regularly check what access apps have and revoke anything unnecessary.

Be cautious on public Wi-Fi

Avoid logging into sensitive accounts on open networks, or use a trusted VPN.

Learn to spot phishing

Urgency, spelling mistakes, and mismatched links are the classic tells.

INCIDENT RESPONSE

If you think you're already infected

Act in this order — it limits the damage and preserves your chances of a clean recovery.

Disconnect from the network

Turn off Wi-Fi or unplug the ethernet cable to stop the malware from spreading or sending data out.

Run a full antivirus scan

Use an updated scanner in safe mode if possible to identify and quarantine the threat.

Change passwords from a clean device

Assume credentials were exposed and update them from a device you trust, starting with email and banking.

Restore from a known-good backup

If files were encrypted or deleted, recovery from an offline backup is far more reliable than paying a ransom.

Get professional help if needed

For ransomware or business systems, a security professional can help you avoid making it worse.
